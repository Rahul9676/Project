# Video Presentation Script & Storyboard
### "Clearing the Air: Who Breathes the Worst Air in New York"
**Target length: 5:00 max · Audience: general public / civic listeners · No code, no jargon**

A briefing, not a code walkthrough. Talk *over* the deployed dashboard (screen-share the
public Streamlit URL), or over the five still images if you prefer. Times are cumulative.

---

### 0:00–0:35 — Hook & the decision context
**On screen:** Dashboard Overview tab, full view. Optionally a photo of the orange June-2023 NYC sky.

> "On a June morning in 2023, New Yorkers woke up to an orange sky. Smoke from Canadian
> wildfires had drifted south, and for a few days the air outside was, by the EPA's own
> scale, hazardous to breathe. That raises a question worth answering with data: how clean
> *is* New York's air, normally — and is everyone breathing the same air? I pulled five years
> of daily readings from eight monitoring stations to find out, because the answer shapes
> where the state should put monitors, warnings, and money."

---

### 0:35–1:30 — Question 1: How clean is the air, most of the time?
**On screen:** KPI strip, then the AQI category bar (Overview, right side).

> "Here's the reassuring part. Most days, the air is genuinely fine — the large majority of
> days rate 'Good' or 'Moderate.' If you only looked at the five-year average, you'd conclude
> New York has a clean, boring air-quality story. But an average is a hiding place. The
> moment you look at the *spread*, a very small number of severe days does most of the damage —
> and that's where the real story is."

**Storytelling beat:** set up the "average hides the truth" tension you'll pay off twice.

---

### 1:30–2:30 — Question 2: Is the air equal across communities?
**On screen:** "City–country gap" bar (Overview), then the station map (Explore).

> "It is not. Sort the stations by community type and a clean gap appears: urban monitors
> average roughly twice the fine-particle load of rural ones, with suburbs in between. On the
> map, you can watch the pollution rise toward the cities — the Bronx and Queens sit at the
> top, a rural Adirondack monitor at the bottom. This is an exposure gap, and it's consistent
> across all five years. Where you live changes the air you breathe."

**Storytelling beat:** this is the "inequality" thread — the heart of the briefing.

---

### 2:30–3:30 — Question 3 & the surprise: What happened in 2023?
**On screen:** "The 2023 smoke event" tab — unhealthy-days-by-year bar.

> "Now the payoff. Count only the *unhealthy* days each year — and four years sit quietly
> around a dozen. Then 2023 jumps to roughly ten times that. That single bar is the wildfire
> smoke, and it's the most important thing in the whole dataset — yet the five-year average
> barely flinches. That's the lesson of exploring data instead of summarizing it: the
> average said 'nothing to see here,' and the spread said 'a public-health emergency happened.'"

**Storytelling beat:** pay off the tension from 1:30. This is your climax.

---

### 3:30–4:15 — Honesty: limits of the data
**On screen:** "Data & sustainability" tab — data-quality notes.

> "Two quick caveats, because good analysis shows its work. First, these are eight fixed
> monitors — not a wall-to-wall map — so a neighborhood without a monitor is invisible here.
> Second, a few readings are obviously broken: values near 900, which no daily average could
> really be, plus a handful of slightly-negative numbers. I flagged those as instrument noise
> rather than pretending they're real. Trust the pattern, interrogate the maximum."

---

### 4:15–5:00 — Recommendations & close
**On screen:** back to Overview, slow zoom on the trend line.

> "So what do we *do* with this? Three things. One: put protection where the exposure is —
> urban communities carry the heaviest everyday load. Two: build for the spikes, not the
> average — a fast public-warning system for smoke episodes matters more than the calm
> baseline suggests. And three: keep this living — the dashboard refreshes the moment a newer
> EPA file is dropped in, so next year's smoke season is one upload away.
> Most days, New York's air is clean. The job is being ready for the days it isn't. Thank you."

---

## Recording tips
- Screen-record the **deployed** dashboard and actually move a filter once on camera — it
  proves interactivity.
- Keep slides/words off; let your voice narrate the visuals.
- Practice once to land under 5:00 — the script above runs ~4:50 at a calm pace.
- Export 1080p, mp4. Free tools: Loom, OBS, or QuickTime + your browser.
